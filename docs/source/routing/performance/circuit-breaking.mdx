---
title: Circuit Breaking
subtitle: Circuit breaking provides critical benefits for system stability, resource efficiency, and graceful failure management in distributed systems when working with federated GraphQL architectures.
---

Circuit breaking is a design pattern used to prevent cascading failures in distributed systems, particularly when dealing with subgraphs. It involves temporarily halting requests to a service that is failing or experiencing high latency, allowing it time to recover without being overwhelmed by additional requests.

## Key points on circuit breaking

Purpose: Circuit breaking is used to maintain system stability by preventing a failing service from affecting the entire system. It helps in managing failures gracefully and ensures that resources are not wasted on requests that are likely to fail.

Implementation: Circuit breakers can be implemented at various levels, such as at the router level for subgraphs. This involves monitoring the health of services and, upon detecting failures or timeouts, temporarily removing the service from the request chain.

Configuration: Circuit breakers can be configured to handle specific scenarios, such as setting thresholds for timeouts or errors. They can also be manually overridden to force a breaker closed or open.

Use Cases: Circuit breaking is particularly useful in environments where services are highly dynamic and can experience varying loads. It is often used in conjunction with other patterns like rate limiting and load balancing to optimize resource usage.

Challenges: Implementing circuit breaking requires careful consideration of the system architecture and the specific needs of the services involved. It may involve using service meshes or custom solutions depending on the infrastructure.

## Options

There are several options for implementing circuit breaking, but there is no single out-of-the-box Apollo solution today. Here’s an overview of the available approaches, their tradeoffs, and real-world usage:

### Service Meshes and Proxies
Service meshes like Istio and proxies such as Envoy and NGINX provide robust circuit breaking at the network or application level.
These tools can be configured to monitor health, error rates, and latency, and to halt or reroute traffic to failing subgraphs or services.
Many organizations already use these for rate limiting and load balancing, and they're generally recommended for circuit breaking outside the Apollo Router.
Service mesh circuit breaking is typically not GraphQL-aware, and may treat entire subgraphs atomically, lacking per-resolver or per-query granularity.

### Custom Router Plugins or Builds
Some users have implemented their own circuit breaking logic as custom plugins or by forking the Apollo Router.
These plugins can use libraries like nodeshift/opossum or Rust crates such as failsafe to implement circuit breaker state machines, with configurable policies (e.g., consecutive failures, success rate over a window, backoff strategies).
This approach allows for more GraphQL-specific logic, such as subgraph-level circuit breaking, and can be tailored to organizational needs, and requires engineering investment to build and maintain, and may not be supported by Apollo directly.

### Application-Level (Datasource/Resolver) Circuit Breaking
Some teams wrap their data sources or resolvers with circuit breaker logic, using libraries such as opossum (Node.js) or resilience4j (Java).
This can be done in Apollo Server or in the subgraph services themselves, allowing for per-resolver or per-backend API circuit breaking.
This approach is decentralized and may require coordination and duplication across services.
